# Midweek Market Recap — April 2, 2026
** Josiah Collins | Sansar Karki Channel**

---

[SECTION: 1 — IRAN CEASEFIRE RALLY]

So this week in the markets was basically a geopolitical roller coaster, and we're only on Thursday.

It started with absolute euphoria on Wednesday. The S&P 500 shot up 0.72% to close at 6,575. The Nasdaq climbed 1.16% to 21,840. And the Dow added 224 points — finishing at 46,565.

Why? Because President Trump posted on Truth Social saying Iran's president asked for a ceasefire. He even said he expects U.S. military forces out of Iran in "two to three weeks." And get this — Trump said there was "regime change." He wasn't even trying to do regime change as a goal. His goal was to make sure Iran doesn't have nuclear weapons. And apparently, that's been accomplished.

Oil dropped on the news. WTI fell 1.24% to settle at $100.12 a barrel. Brent crude lost 2.7% to close at $101.16. For context, oil had been spiking hard because of the conflict — so the market smelled blood in the water and rallied hard.

TheStreet reported the S&P was up 0.69% on the day. Gold jumped about $100 to $4,748. Bitcoin climbed to $68,448. It was a broad-based risk-on day across basically every asset class.

[SECTION: 2 — OIL SHOCK REVERSAL]

And then Thursday happened.

In one speech, Trump threw all that optimism right out the window.

He gave a national address and said the U.S. would continue hitting Iran "extremely hard" for the next two to three weeks. No mention of a timeline to de-escalate. Just flat-out escalation.

The market did not handle that well.

The S&P 500 dropped 1.1%. The Dow fell 545 points — that's 1.2%. And the Nasdaq took the hardest hit, down 1.6%. Nearly three out of every four stocks in the S&P were trading lower. This is a broad-based selloff, not a sector rotation.

Here's the kicker: oil is now trading above $110.

WTI surged 11.7% in a single day to $111.92 per barrel. Brent crude jumped 7.8% to $109.10. This is what happens when you have a major oil-producing region in an active war and the President says he's not de-escalating. The Strait of Hormuz — which handles about one-fifth of the world's traded oil — has seen severe disruption. That supply shock is hitting markets hard.

Energy stocks actually climbed. Exxon gained 2.3%, Chevron advanced 3.4%. If you owned oil majors this week, you were protected. Everyone else? Not so much.

Airlines got absolutely clobbered. United Airlines fell 6.1%. Carnival dropped 5%. When oil rips like this, travel stocks get crushed because fuel costs are everything for them.

The worst part — yes, there is a worse part — markets had briefly approached a 10% correction from all-time highs earlier this week before the rally. So we've basically been through a correction scare, a huge ceasefire rally, and an escalation crash all in four days. That's genuinely insane volatility.

Also worth noting: markets are closed Friday for Good Friday. So today is the last trading day of the week, and we're finishing it in the red.

[SECTION: 3 — SPACEX IPO FILING]

Alright, now for something completely different.

SpaceX — yes, that SpaceX — filed confidentially for an IPO this week.

CNBC confirmed it. Bloomberg broke it. And apparently SpaceX is targeting a valuation of $1.75 trillion. Let me say that again: $1.75 trillion. With a T. This would be the biggest IPO in U.S. history by an absurd margin. Alibaba raised $22 billion back in 2014. SpaceX is reportedly looking to raise up to $75 billion. Three times bigger than the biggest IPO ever.

The company merged with xAI back in February, which Musk valued at $1.25 trillion at the time. And SpaceX has raked in over $24 billion in government contracts since 2008 — NASA, Air Force, Space Force. They've done 165 orbital flights in 2025 alone. They're launching NASA's biggest missions.

If this IPO actually happens — and that's still a big if — Musk becomes the first person to lead two separate trillion-dollar public companies. He already has Tesla at around $1.4 trillion. And his net worth is closing in on $840 billion according to Forbes. He's not just rich. He's a different species of rich.

One quote from a Georgetown finance professor stuck with me: "Anyone who wants more exposure to Elon Musk — this is their opportunity to get in."

Whether that's a good thing or a terrifying thing is a separate debate.

[SECTION: 4 — ELI LILLY GLP-1 PILL APPROVED]

And the fourth story this week — less dramatic geopolitically, but potentially huge for healthcare investors.

Eli Lilly got FDA approval for Foundayo. That's their GLP-1 weight loss pill. It was approved on Wednesday and starts shipping through Lilly's direct-to-consumer platform on Monday.

This matters because it's the first GLP-1 pill — not an injection, a pill — approved for obesity. In clinical trials, the highest dose delivered an average of 12.4% weight loss. That's not a rounding error. That's real, clinically significant weight loss in a once-a-daily pill with no food or water restrictions.

Novo Nordisk launched their oral GLP-1 just three months ago. So now we're in the early stages of a direct competition between the two biggest players in the biggest drug market in the world. This is GLP-1 wars: round two.

The investment angle here is huge. The injectable GLP-1 market is already measured in tens of billions. The pill format opens it up to a completely different patient population — people who literally cannot tolerate weekly injections. This is a massive addressable market expansion.

Lilly shares jumped about $40 on the approval news. Watch this space.

[TLDR]

So what did we learn this week?

One: geopolitical headlines move markets faster than anything else right now. A ceasefire tweet drove a 1% Nasdaq rally. One escalation speech reversed it all in a single day.

Two: oil is the pressure point. $111 WTI changes everything for airlines, consumers, and inflation expectations. If this Strait of Hormuz disruption persists, expect the volatility to continue.

Three: SpaceX at $1.75 trillion would be the biggest IPO in U.S. history. That's not a drill. When it comes, it's going to be a historic event in markets.

Four: Eli Lilly just opened a new front in the GLP-1 wars with a pill format. The injectable market is massive. The oral market might be bigger.

This week was a case study in why you don't make big portfolio moves based on geopolitical headlines — because you will get run over.

Stay sharp. Watch oil. Watch Iran. And watch SpaceX when the filing goes public.
